Brewery profile

Shinrai

Miwa Sake Brewery
Rich
Mellow
Fruity

Few breweries have built a fuller food sake range than Shinrai. They draw on two water sources — one hard, one soft — and use brewing methods that pull each in opposite directions. The hard water and rich rice get tempered by the soft water and quieter techniques.

What comes out is a range you don't get tired of pouring. Different richness, different weight, the same fineness on the palate. The kind of sake to put on a dinner table and leave there.

Water

Two wells, two characters

Two wells. Soft water from a 15-meter well, used in the brewery and at the final fermentation stage — it gives the sake its fine texture. Hard water from a 50-meter well, used in the starter and the first two additions. Together: rich flavor without coarseness.

Rice

Jinsekikogen, expressed in every bottle

Miwa-san wants the sake to read as Jinsekikogen — its region. The rice is local: Sendbon Nishiki and Hattan Nishiki, both Hiroshima prefectural strains, contracted directly with the farms.

Terroir

Kimoto, wood, and the brewery's own microflora

Miwa-san is moving more of the production to kimoto, which leans on the brewery's own ambient lactic bacteria and yeast. He's also building wooden fermentation vessels from local wood — the way most sake was made before the 20th century.
