Every term, plain.

142 terms from the sake world, defined without the jargon.

A
Amazake 甘酒
A sweet, low- or no-alcohol drink made from fermented rice. Often served warm at shrines in winter. Not technically sake, but made with the same koji process.
Ingredients
Arabashiri 荒走り
The first sake to flow from the press under its own weight, before any pressure is applied. Typically bolder and rougher than the middle fraction. Some breweries bottle it separately.
Brewing
Atsukan 熱燗
Sake served hot, around 50°C. Best with rich, full-bodied junmai or kimoto styles. The heat opens up umami and rounds off acidity. Not recommended for aromatic ginjo.
Serving
D
Daiginjo 大吟醸
Sake brewed with rice polished to 50% or less of the original grain. The most refined classification. Often aromatic, elegant, and served chilled. "Junmai Daiginjo" means no added alcohol.
Styles
G
Genshu 原酒
Undiluted sake. Most sake is diluted with water after pressing to bring alcohol down from ~18% to ~15%. Genshu skips this step — bolder, more concentrated, higher ABV.
Styles
Ginjo 吟醸
Sake brewed with rice polished to 60% or less. Generally lighter and more aromatic than junmai. The polishing removes proteins and fats that create heavier flavors.
Styles
Gohyakumangoku 五百万石
One of the two most important sake rice varieties in Japan, alongside Yamada Nishiki. Grown primarily in Niigata and the north. Produces clean, crisp, lighter-bodied sake.
Ingredients
J
Junmai 純米
Pure rice sake — made with only rice, water, koji, and yeast. No added brewer's alcohol. Tends to be fuller-bodied with more rice character. No polishing requirement.
Styles
K
Kimoto 生酛
The oldest method of building the yeast starter, using ambient bacteria to produce lactic acid naturally. Takes about a month (vs. two weeks for modern sokujo). Produces richer, more complex sake with higher acidity.
Brewing
Koji 麹
Rice inoculated with Aspergillus oryzae mold. The koji converts starch to sugar, making fermentation possible. The single most important variable in sake production. About 20–30% of the total rice used becomes koji.
Ingredients
Kura 蔵
A sake brewery. Literally "storehouse." The building where sake is made, often centuries old with thick earthen walls that help regulate temperature naturally.
Brewing
M
Muroka 無濾過
Sake that has not been charcoal-filtered (fined). Retains more color, texture, and flavor than filtered sake. Often slightly yellow or gold in appearance.
Styles
N
Nama 生
Unpasteurized sake. Most sake is pasteurized twice; nama skips both. Fresher, livelier, and more volatile — must be refrigerated. Some breweries pasteurize once (nama-chozo or nama-zume).
Styles
Nigori にごり
Cloudy sake, coarsely filtered so rice sediment remains. Ranges from slightly hazy to thick and creamy. Often sweeter and lower in acidity. Shake gently before pouring.
