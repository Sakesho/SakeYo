Brewery Profile

Hakugakusen

Yasumoto Sake Brewery
Rich Style
Mellow Style
Fruity Style

Hakugakusen sake epitomizes the essence of a ginjo style sake. Its satiny texture, balanced profile, and restrained yet soft approach unfold gradually across the palate, intensifying in flavor and complexity. This progression is complemented by a refreshing acidity on the finish, which resets the palate for each sip.

What truly defines the character of this sake is its water source, originating from a mythical spring on Mt. Haku that embarks on a 100-year journey before reaching the bottle. This water, infused with history and purity, shapes its flavor profile and contributes to its exceptional quality.

Water
水

Mt. Haku — a century underground

Hakugakusen's water originates from Mt. Haku, one of Japan's most legendary water sources with GI status. This natural spring water begins as pristine snowmelt, gradually filtering through layers of rock before descending 100 meters to a natural spring below the brewery. Known for its medium-hard composition, this water imparts impeccably clean and pure flavors along with a luxuriously smooth, satin-like texture.

Rice
酒米

Fukui rice, nowhere else

Crafted exclusively from rice sourced from Fukui Prefecture — Gohyakumangoku and Gin no Sato varieties grown in specially designated areas. Fukui is renowned for cultivating high-quality Gohyakumangoku rice, particularly in the Ono area. Sake brewed with it is characterized by its smooth mouthfeel and crisp quality. Gin no Sato has a fragrant aroma and balanced flavor profile, ideal for highly polished junmai daiginjo.

Dancing Acidity
酸度

A finish that resets the palate

The mineral-rich water from Mt. Haku provides the fermentation with nutrients to produce higher acidity levels that balance the sweetness. The brewing process and a proprietary yeast give the acidity nuance — very soft in the beginning, but it builds over the course of the sip, creating wonderful interactions with food. On the finish, its acidity spreads across the palate, resetting for the next bite or sip. Yasumoto-san calls this 'dancing acidity.'
