SAKEYŌ
Seasons

Oysters in Japan are a winter fish. The cold months pull them tight and briny; by April they spawn and turn soft. There is a sake called Yamawa "for oyster" — sharp, dry, brewed for one pairing and one season.

Sake follows this logic everywhere. This is where we write about what is in season — the sake, and what to drink it with.

Field Notes

The bonito runs north in spring and south in fall, and the sake changes with it

Katsuo crosses Japan twice a year. The spring fish — hatsugatsuo — rides the Kuroshio up past Kochi in March, lean and almost grassy. The autumn fish — modorigatsuo — comes back down in September fat with summer feeding, the belly streaked the color of cured ham. Same animal, not the same dish. Hatsugatsuo wants a sharp junmai served cool. Modorigatsuo wants a hiyaoroshi with weight, served at room temperature so the fat doesn't seize. The Edo poets wrote about hatsugatsuo the way wine writers write about the first Beaujolais. The autumn fish is better.

Shinshu is the sake that smells like the brewery it came from

The kura starts in November. By February the first tanks are pressed, and what comes out is shinshu — new sake, unblended, often unpasteurized, shipped while it still tastes like the room it was made in. There is a smell shinshu has that older sake doesn't: green, faintly of rice paste, of cold concrete and steam. It fades by June. Drink it now or wait a year for something else. Pair it with the first bamboo shoots of the season, simmered in dashi. Pair it with sashimi of madai. Pair it with nothing and drink it standing up in the kitchen at four in the afternoon.

Hiyaoroshi rests through summer and meets the Pacific saury at the door

Hiyaoroshi is sake pressed in late winter, pasteurized once, and left alone in the brewery's coldest room until the outside air matches the temperature inside the tank. That happens in September. It ships without a second pasteurization and sells for about six weeks. It arrives the same week sanma does. Sanma — Pacific saury — runs down the Tōhoku coast in early autumn, silver and oily. You grill it whole over charcoal, salt only, until the skin splits. Grated daikon, a wedge of sudachi, the bitter green guts left in. The hiyaoroshi sits next to it at room temperature and does what a year of rest taught it to do.
